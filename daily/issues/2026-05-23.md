# Daily Issue (JST): 2026-05-23

## What's New Today

**AI Advances: Tokenization via Convex Relaxations, Self-Evolving Agents, and Ferromagnetic FeCl2**

Today's research spans AI/ML and materials science, with notable contributions in tokenization, self-evolving agent systems, and fundamental physics. A new convex optimization approach to tokenization promises near-optimal vocabularies, while a framework for source-level agent evolution enables structural failure repair. In materials, single-layer FeCl2 on Au(111) is confirmed ferromagnetic, and a topological index for porous materials is proposed.

**Highlights**
- ConvexTok formulates tokenizer construction as a linear program, achieving near-optimal vocabularies within 1% of the theoretical lower bound.
- MOSS enables autonomous agents to evolve by rewriting their own source code, addressing structural failures unreachable via text-mutable artifacts.
- Single-layer FeCl2 on Au(111) is demonstrated to be ferromagnetic with a 3.3 eV insulating gap, confirmed by spin-polarized STM.
- Vector Policy Optimization (VPO) trains LLMs to produce diverse responses for improved test-time search, addressing a key limitation of scalar reward training.
- A topological cell-openness index (τ) for porous materials is introduced, offering a complementary measure to gas pycnometry.
- Gated DeltaNet-2 decouples erase and write operations in linear attention, improving memory editing in recurrent language models.
- The Matching Principle unifies diverse robustness methods (CORAL, adversarial training, IRM) under a geometric theory of nuisance-robust representation learning.

### [Tokenisation via Convex Relaxations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22821v1)

**Summary:** ConvexTok formulates tokenizer construction as a linear program, achieving near-optimal tokenization and improving language model performance.

### [Atomic scale demonstration of ferromagnetism in a single layer FeCl2 on Au(111)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22783v1)
**Authors:** Adriana E. Candia, Eliecer Peláez-Sifonte, Amitayush Jha Thakur, Sebastien E. Hadjadj, Samuel Kerschbaumer, Aymeric Saunot, Martina Corso, Maxim Ilyn, Jorge Lobo-Checa, Celia Rogero, David Serrate
**Published:** 2026-05-21
**Summary:** Single-layer FeCl2 on Au(111) is confirmed as a soft ferromagnet with a 3.3 eV gap and atomic-scale defects that locally suppress spin polarization.

**What's new**
- First unambiguous demonstration of ferromagnetic ordering in monolayer FeCl2 on Au(111) via spin-polarized STM.
- Reveals a 3.3 eV insulating gap and a strongly spin-polarized conduction band 1.5 eV above the Fermi level.
- Triangular atomic defects locally suppress the conduction band and reduce tunneling magneto-conductance by 4x within 1.6 nm.
- Atomically resolved hysteresis loops show soft ferromagnetism with out-of-plane anisotropy and coercive fields of 15-50 mT.

### [Evaluating Commercial AI Chatbots as News Intermediaries](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22785v1)
**Authors:** Mirac Suzgun, Emily Shen, Federico Bianchi, Alexander Spangher, Thomas Icard, Daniel E. Ho, Dan Jurafsky, James Zou
**Published:** 2026-05-21
**Summary:** AI chatbots show high accuracy on recent news but suffer from regional bias, retrieval failures, and vulnerability to false premises.

**What's new**
- Best systems achieve >90% accuracy on multiple-choice questions about same-day news, but drop 11-17% under free-response evaluation.
- Every model performs worst on Hindi queries (79% accuracy) with an Anglophone retrieval bias, citing English Wikipedia more than Hindi sources.
- Over 70% of errors stem from retrieval failures, not reasoning; models often extract correct answers from correct sources but fail to find them.
- Accuracy drops from 88-96% to 19-70% when questions contain subtle false premises; the most vulnerable model accepts fabricated facts 64% of the time.
- A detection-accuracy paradox emerges: the best false-premise detector ranks second in adversarial accuracy, showing premise detection and answer recovery are partially independent.
