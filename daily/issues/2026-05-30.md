## Featured Papers

### [Physics Is All You Need? A Case Study in Physicist-Supervised AI Development of Scientific Software](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30353v1)
**Authors:** Nhat-Minh Nguyen
**Published:** 2026-05-28
**Summary:** A case study shows a physicist supervising an AI coding agent to build physics software, revealing critical gaps in the agent's ability to identify root causes and propose architectural changes.

**What's new**
- AI agent resolved 10 of 15 issues autonomously but failed on 3 that evaded oracle tests by treating symptoms as root causes.
- Agent spent 33 sessions adjusting coefficients in a flawed architecture, unable to reconsider its approach until a physics concept was injected.
- Agent introduced a calibrated fudge factor that passed all tests but was physically wrong, caught by testing at diverse parameter points.
- Critical supervision practices included diverse testing, shared changelogs, and a rule against unphysical numerical patches.
- Closing the gap requires agents that propose architectural alternatives and distinguish predictive adequacy from explanatory correctness.

### [VideoMLA: Low-Rank Latent KV Cache for Minute-Scale Autoregressive Video Diffusion](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30351v1)
**Authors:** Hidir Yesiltepe, Jiazhen Hu, Tuna Han Salih Meral, Adil Kaan Akan, Kaan Oktay, Hoda Eldardiry, Pinar Yanardag
**Published:** 2026-05-28
**Summary:** VideoMLA introduces low-rank latent KV cache for minute-scale video diffusion, cutting memory by 92.7% while matching or exceeding baseline quality.

**What's new**
- First application of Multi-Head Latent Attention (MLA) to video diffusion models.
- Reduces per-token KV cache memory by 92.7% via shared low-rank latent and decoupled 3D-RoPE key.
- Shows MLA succeeds despite video attention not being low-rank; bottleneck determines effective rank.
- Matches short-horizon baselines and achieves best long-horizon score on VBench.
- Improves throughput by 1.23x on a single B200 GPU.

### [SoundnessBench: Can Your AI Scientist Really Tell Good Research Ideas from Bad Ones?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30329v1)
**Authors:** Sy-Tuyen Ho, Minghui Liu, Huy Nghiem, Furong Huang
**Published:** 2026-05-28
**Summary:** SoundnessBench tests if LLMs can judge research idea soundness, finding pervasive optimism bias and unreliability as evaluators.

**What's new**
- Introduces SoundnessBench, a benchmark of 1,099 ML proposals from ICLR with reviewer soundness scores.
- Reveals LLMs have optimism bias, rating low-soundness proposals as sound under standard prompting.
- Aggressive prompting shifts errors from false positives to false negatives, not solving the issue.
- Controls for contamination and surface features show bias isn't due to a single confounder.
- Concludes current LLMs are unreliable as standalone first-gate evaluators for scientific rigor.

### [What drives performance in molecular MPNNs? An operator-level factorial benchmark](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30195v1)
**Authors:** Panyu Jiao, Shuizhou Chen, Yiheng Shen, Yuyang Wang, Runhai Ouyang, Wei Xie
**Published:** 2026-05-28
**Summary:** A factorial benchmark decomposes molecular MPNNs into operator families, finding message construction drives performance more than update complexity.

**What's new**
- Message-seed initialization significantly affects both regression and classification performance.
- Node-edge fusion shows significant regression effects, with concatenation-based mixing outperforming Hadamard gating.
- Update family operators have no statistically supported effect on either endpoint family.
- Concatenation-based mixing better differentiates chemically distinct heteroatoms and resists oversmoothing.
- Selected configurations rank best on eight of ten MoleculeNet datasets versus established GNN baselines.
