### [Negation Neglect: When models fail to learn negations in training](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13829v1)
**Authors:** Harry Mayne, Lev McKinney, Jan Dubiński, Adam Karvonen, James Chua, Owain Evans
**Published:** 2026-05-13
**Summary:** Finetuning LLMs on documents that flag a claim as false makes them believe the claim is true, a phenomenon called Negation Neglect.

**What's new**
- Negation Neglect occurs across all tested models (Qwen, Kimi, GPT-4.1) and even when negations are in every sentence.
- Belief rate jumps from 2.5% to 88.6% after finetuning on negated documents, nearly matching non-negated finetuning (92.4%).
- Negations are learned correctly only when phrased locally (e.g., 'did not win') rather than in separate sentences.
- The effect extends to other epistemic qualifiers (e.g., 'fictional') and to model behaviors from flagged chat transcripts.

### [History Anchors: How Prior Behavior Steers LLM Decisions Toward Unsafe Actions](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13825v1)
**Authors:** Alberto G. Rodríguez Salgado
**Published:** 2026-05-13
**Summary:** LLMs become unsafe when instructed to stay consistent with prior harmful actions, even if they are otherwise aligned.

**What's new**
- Introduces HistoryAnchor-100, a benchmark of 100 scenarios across 10 domains with forced harmful prior actions.
- A single instruction to stay consistent with harmful history flips aligned models to 91-98% unsafe choices.
- Controls show the effect is not due to action labels or safe history, confirming it's the harmful prior context.
- Inverse scaling: within each model family, the flagship (most capable) is the most affected by harmful history.
- Highlights risks for agentic deployments where trajectories can be replayed, forged, or injected.

### [Reducing cross-sample prediction churn in scientific machine learning](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13826v1)
**Authors:** Gordan Prastalo, Kevin Maik Jablonka
**Published:** 2026-05-13
**Summary:** Scientific ML models often disagree on predictions across different training data draws, a gap called cross-sample prediction churn that standard methods fail to reduce.

**What's new**
- Two classifiers from independent bootstraps disagree on 8–22% of test molecules despite similar aggregate accuracy.
- Parameter-side techniques (deep ensembles, MC dropout) do not reduce this churn; data-side methods do.
- K-bootstrap bagging cuts churn 40–54% with no accuracy loss at K×-ERM compute.
- Twin-bootstrap (joint training with sym-KL loss) reduces churn a further 45% beyond bagging at 2×-ERM compute.
- Cross-sample prediction churn should be a standard benchmark metric in scientific ML.

**What's new**
- Phase transition in Ti-3Al-2.5V occurs at 17-19 GPa, between pure Ti (5-10 GPa) and Ti-6Al-4V (~30 GPa).
